Tesaro Trial For Chemo Side-Effects Drug Disappoints

Mon, Dec 23 2013 00:00:00 E 00_WEB

By MICHAEL KREY, INVESTOR'S BUSINESS DAILY

 Posted 12/23/2013 11:20 AM ET

  • Email
  • Print
  • License
  • Comment

Tesaro stock plunged more than 20% in morning trading in the stock market today after the oncology-focused biopharmaceutical company reported drug trial results.

The Waltham, Mass.-based company said secondary endpoints for phase III trials of rolapitant were not met, though primary endpoints were met. Rolapitant is being developed to treat chemotherapy-induced nausea and vomiting, or CINV, for cancer patients.

The company said it remains on track to make an NDA, or new drug application, submission for oral rolapitant to the U.S. Food and Drug Administration. It says it's enrolling patients receiving highly emetogenic chemotherapy, or HEC, for a third and final phase III trial of rolapitant.

CINV is estimated to afflict more than 70% of cancer patients undergoing chemotherapy and can result in a delay or discontinuation of chemotherapy treatment because of prolonged nausea and vomiting.

"We are enthusiastic about the potential for this product candidate," Tesaro President Mary Hedley said in the company's press release.

The phase III trial tested 1,369 cancer patients receiving moderately emetogenic chemotherapy, or MEC, and 555 patients receiving HEC.

"The primary endpoint was successfully achieved in each trial," the company said.

Tesaro stock was down 24% in morning trading Monday, near 28.70, the lowest its been since May. The lightly traded stock is still up nearly 70% for the year, however.

In March, Tesaro completed a secondary offering of 5.43 million shares, at $18 a share, netting $91.2 million in proceeds after expenses.

It made its $81 million initial public offering in June 2012, pricing shares at $13.50. Its venture backers reportedly included such big names as New Enterprise Associates, Kleiner Perkins Caufield & Byers, and InterWest Partners.

It's also developing two other oncology-related drugs.
