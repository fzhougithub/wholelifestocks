Riverbed Underrated, Or Perpetually Underperforming?

Dec. 19, 2013 2:51 PM ET |  by: Stephen Simpson, CFA About: RVBD

Management at Riverbed Technology (RVBD) has definitely put forth the effort to try to rally the troops and get the sell-side to take a more positive view of the company's growth potential. Given real issues in WAN optimization demand, not to mention a history of blaming "sales execution" for weak quarters and a disappointingly slow integration of Opnet, I can understand why the Street is responding with a "show me" valuation on these shares.

I have my doubts about both the quality of management at Riverbed and the company's overall business plan. While demand for performance optimization and management is not going away, I do have my doubts about how Riverbed will fare against the likes of Cisco (CSCO), F5 (FFIV), and Citrix (CTXS) as the market evolves. If you believe that Riverbed can do what it says it will and deliver high single-digit free cash flow growth, though, these shares do look undervalued enough today to be worth a close look.

WAN Optimization - Renaissance, Or False Dawn?

Riverbed generates about 70% of its revenue from its WAN optimization segment, including both Steelhead appliances and Granite storage delivery appliances. While WAN optimization was a significant opportunity in the days when enterprises relied upon complex client/server apps, the migration to browser-based apps has led to a significant slowdown in the WAN optimization market and repeated disappointments in Riverbed's performance and guidance.

Now the question is whether there's a better opportunity here than commonly thought by the Street. Implementation of Microsoft's (MSFT) Office 365 appears to be driving a rebound in Steelhead demand, and management believes that greater cloud adoption will likewise spur better than expected demand for WAN optimization products.

I'll grant that it's possible, but I have my doubts. WAN/Granite sales were up just 1% in the third quarter and I'm inclined to go with Gartner's view that WAN optimization isn't a market with much growth potential anymore. Perhaps Riverbed will log some Steelhead sales tied to Office 365 or migration to Amazon's (AMZN) AWS), but I believe any improvements will be short-lived. I also believe that Cisco's strategy of pushing increasingly integrated routers will put pressure on Riverbed.

I'm more bullish on the potential for Granite, and the possibility of Riverbed leveraging Granite customers (about one-third of whom are otherwise new to Riverbed) into expanded sales, but I don't believe Granite is large enough to make a significant difference.

Both ADC And Opnet Face Significant Rivals

Riverbed has been reporting good growth from its Stingray virtual ADC platform, with sales up 53% in the third quarter. Virtual ADCs have definitely changed the ADC market, and market leader F5 was arguably caught off-balance by the speed of adoption in the market. Still, F5 has responded with its own virtual ADC platform and both F5 and Citrix can offer a broader array of ADC solutions (both physical and virtual) than Riverbed.

Turning to Opnet, management claimed during its November analyst day that the integration of this acquisition was finally fully complete. As Opnet is critical to the company's vision of becoming an end-to-end performance management and optimization company, that's no small detail.

I do like the growth potential of the application/network performance management market, and I do believe that Opnet's platform and solution was a natural fit with Riverbed's preexisting business. Once again, though, I'm concerned about competition. Rivals like F5 and Cisco have targeted many of the same markets as Riverbed, and I likewise wonder whether Opnet will manage to out-compete IBM (IBM), Oracle (ORCL), or Hewlett-Packard (HPQ) over the long term. I like the near-term prospects for converting at least some of the large installed base of Steelhead customers into Opnet/Riverbed Performance Management customers, but I think management's expectation of share-gaining double-digit revenue growth may be too ambitious.

Strategic M&A Seems Unlikely, But You Never Know

Riverbed has attracted some attention as a potential buyout candidate, helped in no small part by the presence of activist investor Elliott Management as a major shareholder. Although Riverbed isn't terrifically expensive today and it is possible to tell a good story about the long-term growth potential in location-independent computing, I'm skeptical.

Rumors have included Cisco and EMC (EMC) as potential bidders. Riverbed would not seem to offer the overall growth potential or segment leadership that EMC typically prizes, and I doubt Riverbed would sell them Granite (arguably the most interesting part to them). Cisco is a popular rumored buyer across the tech space and given the company's historical willingness to do some odd deals, I suppose it can't be ruled out. That said, given the historical execution issues at Riverbed I would think a financial buyer (private equity) would be the more likely candidate if there were a deal to be done.

The Bottom Line

Riverbed stays on my watch list primarily because I've long thought that the company could eventually sort out its operational and execution challenges and generate some solid growth. Likewise, I won't completely dismiss the possibility that the company's new end-to-end management and optimization strategy will drive a sustained period of good results. The key, as it has long been, is executing on that potential.

I'm looking for long-term revenue and free cash flow growth of 8% to 9%, which is meaningfully more than I expect from the likes of Cisco or F5. With that, the shares would have a fair value above $20 and that's enough to keep my attention. I definitely have "trust issues" when it comes to accepting Riverbed's management projections of growth, but the Street's skepticism may just be setting up a market-beating bargain here.
