Bring Back Our Dollars: Lululemon’s Overseas Cash is Coming Home

Lululemon Athletica Inc. made an about-face this year in how it thinks about its foreign cash.

In a February filing with securities regulators, Lululemon Athletica Inc. said its foreign cash would be held abroad “for the foreseeable future.”

The foreseeable future ended up being May when the yoga-gear maker decided it needed most of that cash at home to pay for a $450-million share buyback program. The company Thursday said would bring $500 million back to the U.S. and take a $30.9 million tax hit that comes with repatriating the cash.

American companies are holding more of their cash offshore in an effort to shield those funds from U.S. taxes. About 1,700 U.S. companies excluding banks held $1.5 trillion in cash off shore at the end of last year, a sum that has nearly doubled since 2008, according to ratings company Standard & Poor’s. To bring that cash home, the companies would have to pay the difference between the 35% federal U.S. tax rate and foreign taxes paid.

Designating earnings as permanently or indefinitely reinvested overseas lets the company avoid accruing U.S. taxes on their financial statements. But as soon as executives decide they want to use those funds at home, they have to set aside provisions for the tax hit.

That’s what Lululemon did. And in April, eBay Inc. said it would shift as much as $9 billion to the U.S., potentially incurring $3 billion in taxes. Avon Products Inc. last year recorded a $169 million tax provision so it could bring back foreign-held cash more easily.

A Lululemon spokesman didn’t immediately comment Friday afternoon.

Company executives have a lot of leeway when designating overseas earnings as reinvested overseas or subject to repatriation at some point in the future. And they can change their minds within reason, says Bret Wells, an assistant law professor at the University of Houston, who teaches tax law and previously served as chief tax officer or tax counsel at large companies.

That said, if a company were to change its mind and repatriate indefinitely reinvested earnings every six months or two years, its auditor might raise questions about the decision not to accrue U.S. taxes on the earnings. Calling earnings permanently reinvested “does not lock you in — there is no legal reason you can’t bring it back,” Mr. Wells says.

Lululemon isn’t bringing back all of its cash held abroad where the company is trying to expand its reach.

“The remaining undistributed earnings of the foreign subsidiaries will be permanently reinvested for the foreseeable future,” the company said.
