Endo Buyout Of Auxilium Joins Two Changing Firms

Fri, Oct 10 2014 00:00:00 E A04_IT

By AMY REEVES, INVESTOR'S BUSINESS DAILY

 Posted 10/09/2014 07:00 PM ET

  • Email
  • Print
  • Reprints
  • Comment

Endo International inked a deal to buy fellow specialty drugmaker Auxilium Pharmaceuticals Thursday after raising its initial bid by nearly 20% to $2.6 billion in cash and stock, or $33.25 a share. Auxilium's shares rose 9% to 32.44, while Endo's stock fell 5.9% to 66.78.

Endo (NASDAQ:ENDP) first approached Auxilium (NASDAQ:AUXL) in mid-September, and on Sept. 16 it announced a $2.2 billion offer, sending Auxilium's stock up nearly 45% the next day. Auxilium rejected the offer a few days later as too low — a sentiment echoed by many analysts — but the relationship became friendlier.

"Our discussions with Endo had been very positive, and the two teams have got to know each other and gained a deeper understanding of our respective businesses that I believe will allow the combined company to hit the ground running upon the closing of the deal," Auxilium CEO Adrian Adams said on a conference call to discuss the buyout.

The deal is designed to solve problems for both companies. Auxilium, which specializes in men's health products, was a leading stock in its group as recently as March. But this year, it's faced declining sales of its lead product, testosterone gel Testim, and sales may not recover since an FDA panel last month advised cracking down on use of testosterone products for less-than-urgent reasons.

It has a few other products that analysts consider promising, notably the fast-acting impotence drug Stendra (licensed from Vivus (NASDAQ:VVUS) and Xiaflex, a drug approved for treating a couple of rare conditions but that might have broader applications. Both products are new, and their sales need to ramp up considerably to make up for Testim.

In June, Auxilium responded by arranging to buy small Canadian ophthalmology firm QLT (NASDAQ:QLTI) in a stock swap. The deal would have allowed Auxilium to redomicile to Canada, which would cut its unusually high 39% tax rate. And on Sept. 9, it announced a plan to cut $75 million in operating costs, including cutting its workforce by 30%.

The stock still stayed low enough to tempt an opportunistic buyer, and Endo became that thanks to its own transformation.

Lidoderm Patent Protection

Endo was traditionally focused on pain medicine, but last year its flagship product Lidoderm lost its patent protection. Company revenue fell 7% last year and continued falling this year.

In March 2013, Endo appointed a new CEO, Rajiv De Silva, who had just spent three years as president of Valeant Pharmaceuticals International (NYSE:VRX). De Silva has been implementing a Valeant-like strategy of cost control and diversifying through acquisitions.
