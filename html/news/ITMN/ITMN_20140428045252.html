    InterMune Lung Drug Is Successful in New Trial

    By ANDREW POLLACKFEB. 25, 2014

    Inside
      Continue reading the main story
      Continue reading the main story
      Continue reading the main story Share This Page
      Continue reading the main story
      Continue reading the main story

      A drug being developed to treat a fatal scarring of the lungs has succeeded in an important clinical trial and could become the first medicine to be approved for that disease, known as idiopathic pulmonary fibrosis.

      The drug, pirfenidone, slowed the decline in lung function in patients with the disease, according to an announcement on Tuesday by the drug’s developer, InterMune. The company said it would apply to the Food and Drug Administration for approval of the drug early in the third quarter of this year.

      The results “met or exceeded my expectations on every metric,” Daniel G. Welch, the chief executive of InterMune, told analysts. The price of InterMune shares rose about 170 percent, closing at $37.80.

      Dr. Daniel M. Rose, chief executive of the Pulmonary Fibrosis Foundation, a patient advocacy group, said the news was encouraging.

      “This disease is deadlier than 60 to 70 percent of malignancies,” Dr. Rose said. Pirfenidone “does not cure the disease, it does not work in every patient, but there is a cohort of patients who do receive a benefit.”

      Idiopathic pulmonary fibrosis, which has no known cause, affects about 70,000 Americans, according to InterMune, though the advocacy group estimates the number could be as high as 200,000. The disease kills many people within two to five years as progressive scarring of the lungs makes it impossible to breathe. Steroids and some other drugs are sometimes used off-label to treat it.

      The F.D.A. declined to approve pirfenidone in 2010 because the drug worked in one clinical trial but not in another study that was nearly identical. The agency requested that InterMune run another trial. That trial’s results were announced on Tuesday.

      Regulators in Europe and Canada, however, did approve the drug based on the earlier trials, and it is sold in those places under the name Esbriet. It has also been sold in Japan since 2008 by Shionogi Inc. under the name Pirespa.

      The F.D.A.’s reluctance to approve the drug the first time could leave it open to complaints that it unnecessarily delayed a treatment option for desperate patients.

      Dr. Rose, of the Pulmonary Fibrosis Foundation, which has received contributions from InterMune and other drug companies, said some patients were upset and some had been ordering pirfenidone from abroad. But over all, he said, “there hasn’t been a huge amount of anger at the F.D.A.”

      The new trial involved 555 patients in the United States and eight other countries. The main endpoint was the change in a measure of lung function called forced vital capacity.

      After one year, 22.7 percent of patients who received the drug had experienced no decline in lung function, compared with only 9.7 percent of the patients who got a placebo. And only 16.5 percent of patients who got the drug had a decline of 10 percent or more in lung function or died. Among those who received a placebo, 31.8 percent had that big a decline or died.

      The drug also slowed the decline in the distance the patients could walk in six minutes, InterMune said.

      Pirfenidone also appeared to help patients live longer. That survival benefit was not statistically significant for the new trial alone. But when the results from the new trial and the two older trials were combined, there was a statistically significant reduction in mortality.

      Pirfenidone can have side effects like nausea, rash and liver toxicity. It is taken three times a day, three capsules each time.

      The results were announced in a news release and had not been peer-reviewed. InterMune left out details, saying it did not want to jeopardize publication of the results in a medical journal.

      Boehringer Ingelheim is expected to announce soon what are rumored to be positive Phase 3 clinical trial results of its own experimental treatment for idiopathic pulmonary fibrosis, a pill called nintedanib. Biogen Idec, Gilead Sciences and others are also testing drugs for the disease.

      Esbriet is the only product marketed by InterMune, which is based in Brisbane, Calif. Revenue from the drug was about $70.3 million in 2013.
