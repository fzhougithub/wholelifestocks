    Out From Alibaba: David Wei Thrives Anew In China

    1 comments, 0 called-out
    Comment Now
    Follow Comments Following Comments Unfollow Comments
    Comment Now
    Follow Comments Following Comments Unfollow Comments

    It was three years ago when David Wei suddenly was out as CEO of Alibaba.com Alibaba.com, bearing responsibility for the sale of fakes at the Chinese e-commerce site that has since rolled on toward a looming huge public offering.  But don’t feel too sorry for him.

    The 43-year-old Wei is thriving again.  He chairs Vision Knight Capital of Shanghai, which has raised $800 million from investors that include the man who sent him packing, Alibaba founder Jack Ma.  Also invested in its internet-oriented firm is Hong Kong billionaire Richard Li’s telecommunications firm PCCW.

     Wei says his “resignation” along with Alibaba’s chief operating officer at the time was a ‘right decision’ for a burgeoning company working out the kinks. He has remained tight with Ma. But he doesn’t want Vision Knight to be seen an arm of Alibaba Group.  

      “In China, we want ourselves to be neutral” in doing business among the “big boys” in the country’s internet universe, a group that also includes search leader Baidu Baidu and game giant Tencent, he says.  “Otherwise, we will miss some opportunities.”

    Vision Knight’s investors made money when Baidu last year paid $1.9 billion for one of its investments, app store 91 Wireless, in China’s biggest Internet acquisition to date.  They also profited when Baidu paid $370 million last year for the video business of PPS, another portfolio company.

    The strategy is built around  the rising influence of Chinese born after 1985. “The post-85 demography is key to everything for us” because of that group’s passion for online spending, Wei says. He believes this will reshape three industries: cars, home furnishings and real estate.  “Some industries can be 100% digitalized, and since they can be 100% digitalized, they should not exist offline,” Wei says.  

    By 2015, e-commerce transactions in China are projected to reach $540 billion, or approximately 10 percent of total retail, and by 2020, China’s e-commerce market should be larger than those of the US, Britain, Japan, Germany, and France combined, according to a recent KPMG report.

    Wei was running the China business for B&Q, the do-it-yourself chain, when he first met Ma in 2000.  “He was inviting everybody to join Alibaba,” Wei recalls, with  words like “This is the new world! ”  Wei stuck with B&Q, but the company’s UK parent Kingfisher Kingfisher started to outsource products from Alibaba’s Taobao.com site, and  he was impressed with the growth Ma was able to show there, doubling business in three-month periods. “I was asking myself, ‘Am I working for a dinosaur?’ Unfortunately, it took seven years to convince myself that e-commerce worked.”

    Before going to Alibaba, however, Wei says he paused to consider starting a private-equity company like the one he runs now. He passed, on the advice of his current partner, Zhu Daming, who told him: “If you missed the Internet at age 35, you will be too old but at age 35, you’re too young to do private equity.  In private equity, age is value. In the Internet, age may be negative.”

    In 2011, he was ready for that next stage, with an American equity firm as a sort of model. “What would happen if Bain Consulting never split with Bain Capital? If they were really, really one company instead of just sharing the name? “Vision Knight differentiates itself among investment companies by offering free consulting support and in turn getting a chance to invest – at a discount to the company’s valuation — if performance improves.

    Its first investment – apparel retailer Cartelo Crocodile – took Vision Knight’s advice with a management buyout and new web strategy.  “David had a lot of good ideas about e-commerce,” which now account for half of sales, according to CEO Yi Qianru  At 91 Wireless, Wei’s team brought on users.  And at online lottery site 500.com, Vision Knight helped to boost the mobile take.

     “The overall lottery business is growing like 14-15%, which is outgrowing GDP [officially 7%], and online lottery is growing 50%,” Wei says. Sounds like those early days at Alibaba.
