Nomura: Invest in Chinese Lotteries During the World Cup

Anyone looking into World Cup-related Chinese investments should check out lottery plays.

That’s the message Nomura is passing on to investors. The Japanese investment bank says investors should buy lottery stocks as the world’s largest football event spurs betting on sports in China. It says U.S.-listed 500.com Ltd. and Hong Kong-listed REXLot Holdings Ltd., which operates online lottery site okooo.com, will benefit.

Agence France-Presse/Getty Images

The allure of these stocks is their Internet presence. Nomura says most people are likely to place their bets through the Internet or through mobile apps, since the time difference between China and Brazil means most matches will start when lottery shops have closed. 500.com, a top Chinese betting firm, has partnered with PPTV, a popular peer-to-peer video streaming site, to launch a World Cup marketing campaign, while REXLot’s okooo.com has cooperated with Tencent to promote its lottery business on its WeChat messaging service. Because gambling is illegal in mainland China, these firms are considered lottery firms, although people can make bets on more than just winning tickets.

Nomura also says the World Cup is likely to drive demand for sportswear, accessories and souvenirs, creating a short-term lift for suppliers to global sports brands like shoemaker Yue Yuen Industrial (Holdings) Ltd. and sportswear maker Shenzhou International Group Holdings Ltd. Both stocks went up during the one-month period in the previous World Cups in 2006 and 2010.

While punters are busy betting on football, other top beneficiaries of Chinese betting, say Macau, may see declines. Nomura says large football tournaments like the previous World Cup in 2010 and European Championship in 2012 slowed down VIP growth at Macau casinos by a few percentage points.

Surprisingly, betting on beer stocks isn’t likely to reap dividends for investors, the brokerage says. “No historical evidence proves that the World Cup drives beer sales volume in China or drives up share prices of related brewers,” Nomura said.
